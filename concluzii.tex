Chapter~5 now records an auditable FastTree checkpoint on the full ULB/Kaggle-style CSV loaded by the companion API, complementing---not replacing---the methodological chapters.

The ExplainableFraud companion software supplied an operational counterpoint: RQ1 materialised as threshold-aware UI affordances tied to exported validation metrics for the same split policy as the training dashboard; RQ2 as the \texttt{MetadataWeightedDeviation} mask whose contract explicitly disclaims Shapley efficiency and LIME fidelity; RQ3 as model identifiers, optional validation metadata, and deterministic HTTP contracts amenable to audit replay. No single artefact ``proves'' the theoretical arc, yet in concert they demonstrate that abstract commitments can be instantiated without surrendering narrative discipline about limitations.

Architecturally minimal instantiations reaffirm that explanation layers complement rather than supersede investigator judgment \cite{rudin_stopexplaining2019,fraud_handbook}. Where scores are marketed as probabilities, calibration discipline links numeric rhetoric to observable frequencies \cite{niculescu_caruana_calib2005}. Methodological reproducibility dominates incidental toolchain branding.

\section{Answering the Research Questions}
\begin{description}
    \item[RQ1 (valuation under rarity)] Precision--recall integrals and threshold sweeps foreground minority-class economics where accuracy obscures collapses toward the majority label \cite{davis_goadrich_icml06,fraud_handbook}; ROC summaries quantify ranking discrimination yet do not singularly determine escalation policy under severe skew \cite{davis_goadrich_icml06}.
    \item[RQ2 (explainability semantics)] Cooperative-game attributions and local surrogate philosophies clarify admissible interpretive claims for \emph{reference} toolchains \cite{lime_original,shap_original,slack2020_fooling_lime_shap}; the shipped hybrid mask $e_i(x)$ remains a metadata-weighted deviation summary whose epistemic status must be communicated literally on every dashboard.
    \item[RQ3 (probability artefacts)] Reliability diagrams, recalibration, and drift telemetry remain mandatory whenever scores feed thresholds framed in probabilistic language \cite{niculescu_caruana_calib2005}; under concept drift documented in transactional fraud surveys, $\hat{y}$ and local attributions co-evolve with shifting cohorts rather than converging toward time-invariant guarantees \cite{lucas_jurgovsky_ccfraud_survey2020}.
\end{description}

Analyst-facing overlays that pair $\hat{y}$ with $e(x)$ improve transparency contingent on disciplined communication policies; versioning and reproducible artefacts operationalise reproducibility mandates \cite{xai_survey}.

\section{Practical Implications}
For institutions triaging PCA-masked transactional archives similar to ExplainableFraud, the takeaway is methodological rather than turnkey deployment: asymmetric metrics (ROC \emph{and} precision--recall summaries) deserve paired reporting before executives interpret leaderboard accuracy, especially when asymmetric costs dominate \cite{davis_goadrich_icml06,fraud_handbook}. Explanations should reuse the ExplainableFraud pattern of explicit labelling (\texttt{MetadataWeightedDeviation}), dataset/model documentation hooks inspired by Model Cards/Datasheets \cite{mitchell2019model_cards,gebru2021datasheets}, and segregation between exploratory \texttt{/training} jobs versus promoted scoring bundles.

EU-facing teams additionally inherit AI Act-aligned expectations for conformity documentation on high-risk creditworthiness or biometric use cases adjacent to transactional monitoring \cite{eu_ai_act2024}; anonymised benchmarks cannot substitute legal compliance work, yet they scaffold the argumentative habits supervisory dialogues increasingly demand.

\section{Limitations}
Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, model behavior and explanation quality depend strongly on the representativeness and quality of the available transaction data. Second, local explanation methods provide useful evidence for individual predictions, but they do not establish causal relationships and may be sensitive to modeling assumptions \cite{shap_original}.

Third, operational performance claims must be interpreted in relation to the specific deployment setting, infrastructure, and analyst workflow in which the system is evaluated. Generalization to other institutions or fraud typologies requires additional validation.

\section{Future Work}
Future work can extend this dissertation along four directions. The first direction is broader evaluation across multiple datasets and temporal windows to assess robustness under distribution shifts. The second direction is integrating global explainability views and drift monitoring dashboards for model governance teams.

The third direction is introducing structured analyst feedback loops to support periodic retraining and policy-aware threshold adjustment. The fourth direction is comparative benchmarking of alternative explainability methods and model families under identical operational constraints (now partially scaffolded by the \texttt{/training} ML.NET selectors and CLI exporters, with differentiable networks still deferred pending ONNX/Torch integration), enabling evidence-based selection for production environments.

\section{Final Remarks}
Rather than asserting a universally novel algorithm for fraud scoring, this dissertation consolidates transferable lessons on imbalance geometry, calibrated communication of scores, disciplined interpretation contracts, and the gap between perturbation-heavy XAI folklore and deliberately modest attribution masks instantiated in ExplainableFraud. Engineering artefacts ground those claims in reproducible telemetry (HTTP contracts, deterministic synthetic fallbacks), while empirical rows from the public ULB archive supply a guarded quantitative checkpoint anchored in the project's JSON transcripts.

Reflective synthesis suggests an iterative research agenda bridging the foregoing chapters: whenever empirical tables materially improve, explanatory interfaces should be scrutinised anew for coherence with updated thresholds; conversely, whenever explanatory paradigms expand (for example calibrated Shapley approximants under AML constraints), evaluation protocols must widen beyond scalar PR summaries to encompass faithfulness probes reviewed in Chapter~5. By freezing this dissertation’s narrative scope at tabular stochastic classification supplemented by heuristic masks and rigorous caveats, the author hopes to furnish a transferable scaffold—rather than claim closure—upon which supervisory, statistical, and human-computer interaction communities might converge collaboratively.

\section{Epistemic positioning for examiners and industry readers}
Examiners evaluating master’s-level contributions balance novelty against reproducibility, conceptual depth against engineering polish. This manuscript intentionally foregrounds the former dyad: software supplements exemplify contracts but do not substitute for formal reasoning about imbalance or interpretability limits. Industry readers should analogously resist equating polished dashboards with regulatory sufficiency—supervisory dialogues in the European Economic Area continue alongside GDPR Article~22 guidance \cite{gdpr_text}, the adopted AI Act's tiered conformity obligations \cite{eu_ai_act2024}, and sector-specific regulator letters that interpret model risk jointly with consumer protection mandates. These instruments contextualise transparent methodological prose and documentation habits even when proprietary code cannot circulate publicly.

Future cohorts may extend the present contribution by embedding investigator-centred user studies, longitudinal drift evaluations on partially redacted corporate feeds, or differential-privacy-aware explanation releases. Each extension imposes fresh ethical review obligations beyond the scope of anonymised public corpora but aligns with the dissertation’s normative thesis: explainability and accountability are ongoing collaborative practices, not checkbox artefacts frozen at submission time.
