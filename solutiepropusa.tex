\chapter{Proposed Solution}

\section{Design Objectives and Constraints}
The proposed solution is designed as an end-to-end decision support system for transaction fraud detection that combines predictive modeling with model interpretability capabilities suitable for real operational use. The primary objective is to improve analyst decision quality by providing both a fraud risk score and an explanation of the factors that most influenced each prediction.

A supplementary engineering stance favours consolidating training, inference, and presentation tooling within homogeneous runtime ecosystems when institutional IT policies prescribe it; nonetheless, analytical claims hinge on stochastic properties of $f_\theta$, not toolchain branding \cite{mldotnet_docs}.

From an operational perspective, the methodological architecture must satisfy four constraints: reproducibility of model behavior across environments, traceability of prediction events for audit scenarios, low-latency response during analyst workflows, and modularity to support future model or explanation-method replacement.

\section{System Architecture}
The system architecture follows a layered pattern composed of data, model, explainability, application, and governance layers. This separation reduces coupling between data preparation, model inference, and user-facing interaction logic.

At a high level, transactions map to vectors $x$ via deterministic preprocessing commits. Supervised discriminative estimator $f_\theta$ induces calibrated (or calibrated post-hoc) fraud propensity estimates. Subsequent attribution primitives compress local sensitivity summaries consumable through analyst interfaces irrespective of graphical toolkit.

The architecture also includes an event logging and audit trail mechanism that stores model version, input snapshot identifiers, prediction score, and explanation summary metadata. This design supports post-hoc review of automated recommendations and is aligned with transparency requirements in high-stakes decision systems.

\section{Data Processing and Feature Engineering}
Fraud detection performance depends strongly on data quality, feature consistency, and class imbalance handling strategy \cite{fraud_handbook}. For this reason, preprocessing is implemented as a versioned pipeline with explicit schema contracts. Each transformation step (missing-value policy, categorical encoding, scaling or normalization where required, and temporal aggregation features) is defined declaratively to ensure reproducible training-serving parity.

Formally, each transaction is represented by a feature vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$, and the model computes a fraud risk score:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = f_{\theta}(x), \quad \hat{y} \in [0,1]
\end{equation}
where $\hat{y}$ is interpreted as the estimated probability of fraud and $\theta$ denotes the trained model parameters. A binary decision is then obtained using a configurable threshold $\tau$:
\begin{equation}
\text{decision}(x) =
\begin{cases}
\text{fraud}, & \hat{y} \geq \tau \\
\text{legitimate}, & \hat{y} < \tau
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Feature design is guided by both domain relevance and explainability utility. In addition to predictive value, selected features should remain semantically interpretable by fraud analysts, such as transaction amount patterns, velocity indicators, merchant-risk aggregates, and behavioral deviations relative to customer historical profiles. Highly opaque engineered features are minimized unless they provide substantial incremental predictive benefit.

To address class imbalance, the pipeline incorporates threshold-aware evaluation and may include weighted learning or rebalancing strategies depending on dataset characteristics. The final decision threshold is treated as a business parameter and calibrated based on risk appetite and investigation capacity rather than fixed at a default probability cutoff.

\section{Fraud Detection Model Strategy}
Let $\mathcal{X}\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ and $\mathcal{Y}=\{0,1\}$. Empirical risk minimisation seeks $\theta^\star\in\Theta$ balancing misclassification surrogates under imbalance-aware weights; tree ensembles (additive expansion of shallow decision stumps) and regularised logistic models exemplify tractable hypothesis classes for tabulated fraud benchmarks \cite{fraud_handbook,mldotnet_docs}. Complexity--variance tensions motivate cross-validated ensemble depth control and abstention thresholds informed by ROC/PR geometries rather than nominal accuracy alone.

Model development follows a controlled experimentation protocol: train-validation-test separation, feature ablation checks, and metric selection aligned with imbalanced classification settings (for example, precision, recall, F1-score, and PR-AUC). Because false negatives in fraud detection can carry high financial impact, recall-sensitive analysis is explicitly considered alongside precision and workload constraints.

To support reliable production use, each trained model artifact is versioned and stored with metadata describing training window, feature schema version, hyperparameter configuration, and evaluation summary. This metadata-driven lifecycle allows rollback and comparative analysis between model versions during monitoring or incident review.

\section{Explainability Layer}
Predictive probabilities alone seldom satisfy auditors; explanatory evidence must articulate \emph{how} individual predictors interacted with $\hat{y}$ at instance $x$. Two mathematical idealisations recur in practice.

\paragraph{Local surrogate programmes (LIME family).}
Let $\nu$ be a neighbourhood distribution centred at $x$. LIME seeks interpretable $g \in G$ minimising $\mathbb{E}_{z\sim\nu}[(f_\theta(z)-g(z))^2]$ subject to complexity penalties. Explanations inherit fidelity only within $\nu$; enlarging or mis-specifying $\nu$ yields inconsistent attributions \cite{lime_original,xai_survey}.

\paragraph{Additive attributions (SHAP family).}
Cooperative-game Shapley values yield vectors $\phi(x)\in\mathbb{R}^d$ satisfying \emph{efficiency} $\sum_i \phi_i(x) = f_\theta(x) - \mathbb{E}[f_\theta]$ under stated reference measures, alongside symmetry and dummy axioms \cite{shap_original}. Tree-specific approximations trade exactness for polynomial-time computation but retain additive decomposability valued in audit trails.

\paragraph{Composite hybrid strategy proposed here.}
The methodological stance adopted in this thesis treats global ranked signals (e.g., univariate association strengths, permutation importance summaries) as orientation priors, while local emphasises modulate instance-level deviation from training moments. Formally, one may view per-feature weights $w_i$ combined with z-scores $z_i(x)$ as a pragmatic sensitivity mask:
\begin{equation}
e_i(x) \propto w_i \cdot |z_i(x)|, \qquad \sum_i e_i(x) = 1 \text{ after normalisation,}
\end{equation}
acknowledging that $e(x)$ is \emph{not} a Shapley vector unless further axiomatic constraints are engineered. Analyst communications must label $e(x)$ as indicative influence magnitudes, not causal effects.

\section{Operational Readiness and Governance Hooks}
Information systems realisations may expose $(\hat{y}, e(x), \text{model id})$ through REST services and interactive clients; such engineering choices are secondary to the mathematical contracts above. Regardless of stack, governance requires: (i) immutable logs linking requests to model versions, (ii) masking of sensitive attributes in traces, and (iii) controlled rotation of $\theta$ with regression tests on both scores and explanation stability \cite{gdpr_text,xai_survey}.

\section{Chapter Summary}
This chapter formalised a methodology where discriminative scoring, threshold policies, and structured attributions coexist under explicit probabilistic and axiomatic regimes. Artefactual reproducibility precedes incidental software packaging. The ensuing chapter concentrates on empirical procedures, comparative metrics, and interpretive scrutiny rather than exhaustive code narration.
